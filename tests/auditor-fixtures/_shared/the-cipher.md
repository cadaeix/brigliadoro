# THE CIPHER

_A two-player CC0 micro-RPG about correspondents passing coded messages across distance._

You will need: 2d6, paper, a friend.

You play two correspondents — exiles, scientists, lovers, conspirators — who can only communicate by letter. The post is unreliable. The world is interfering.

---

## Sending

Each turn, one player writes their letter and rolls 2d6. Read the high die.

- **6: Clear** — the letter arrives. The recipient reads it as written.
- **4 or 5: Garbled** — the letter arrives, but parts are missing, smudged, or transposed. The reader picks one phrase and treats it as illegible.
- **2 or 3: Lost** — the letter never arrives. The recipient writes back into the silence.

If both dice match (two ones, two fives, two sixes, any pair), **the cipher is broken**. Whoever's reading the letter knows it. Future letters can't use the cipher; you both have to invent a new code or send openly. Note the broken cipher between you and don't forget it.

After you read the letter, write your reply.

---

## Complications

When the letter arrives, before reading, roll d6 on the complications table:

1. The post was searched. The seal is wrong; someone has read this first.
2. The letter is months late. What it describes is no longer true.
3. The handwriting is not your correspondent's. Someone is replying for them.
4. There is a second letter in the envelope, addressed to you, signed by no-one.
5. The paper is from a place neither of you have been.
6. No complication. The letter arrives as it left.

---

## A worked example

Léon and Marguerite are correspondents in opposite corners of an empire at war with itself. Léon writes to Marguerite about a defection. He rolls 2d6 and gets a 5 and a 5 — Garbled, and the cipher is broken.

The letter arrives. Marguerite reads _"____ has crossed the Vistula ____ no, wait, the city is ____ no ____"_ and the rest is illegible. She also sees that the envelope was opened. She knows the cipher is broken; whoever read this letter has the key.

She has to reply. She can't use the cipher. She writes openly, knowing whoever is reading it will read this too. She writes about the weather.

---

## Voice

Letters are formal. People wrote like this. _Dearest_, _your devoted_, _yours in this and in the next world_. Lean into it. The roleplaying lives in the letters; the dice tell you whether the letters arrive.

The game ends when the correspondence ends — when you decide it has, or when one of you stops writing.
